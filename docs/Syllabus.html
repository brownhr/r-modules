GHY 5800 – Advanced Quantitative Methods in Geography

Syllabus

Course Description

This course provides a critical introduction to quantitative methods commonly used in the analysis of applied geographic research problems. The content of the course is geared towards the discussion and practice of common research techniques and theoretical aspects used to investigate issues in human geography, physical geography, GIScience, and community and regional planning. Specific topics include reliability and validity, research ethics, survey design, case study methods, descriptive statistics, inferential statistics, spatial statistics, univariate statistical methods, multivariate statistical methods, regression analysis, and spatial autocorrelation. Adjustments to the methods covered may be made per the composition of the class on a semester by semester basis.

This course will be structured like a survey of research methods. As we survey the methods, we will read practical “how-to” literature, but we will also collectively seek out examples of well-done research using these tools, and trace the ways that scholars have used these methods, including a discussion of how they built their argument around specific modes of analysis. Readings will be provided by the instructor and active participation in discussion by all students is expected during class meetings. Mindful reading and participation are therefore critical to discussion and exploration of research in each student’s area of interest. Students are expected to read all materials prior to class and prepare weekly comments or questions on each week’s reading.

Learning Outcomes

Specific course objectives include:

  1. Building a knowledge-base and toolkit of quantitative techniques that enable students to appropriately collect and analyze data
  2. Developing a professional research project demonstrating research methods skills
  3. Enabling students to confidently and carefully interpret the results of data analyses and clearly communicate those results
  4. Exploring how to design studies to collect appropriate data for addressing research questions
  5. Improving writing, communications, critical thinking, and analytical skills within the context of the course content

Course Requirements and Grading

  • 20% Assignments / Lab Exercises
  • 30% Final Project
  • 15% Seminar Leadership
  • 10% Participation
  • 12.5% Final Exam
  • 12.5% Midterm

Weekly Assignments and Lab Exercise

Weekly assignments will account for 20% of the final grade in this course. These will consist of readings, discussion assignments, and/or in-class assignments as well as lab exercises. The lab exercises are an integral part of the quantitative methods portion of this course. The labs are designed to support our discussions in the lecture by providing application-based examples of the numerous quantitative analysis tools that we will be discussing. Weekly readings are also required. Students must prepare a discussion assignment related to the reading and bring a copy to class.

Seminar Leadership

We will alternate the leadership of the seminar throughout the semester. Seminar leaders will guide us through the readings and provoke discussion on the topic at hand. This should include doing a little research to find out the disciplinary backgrounds of the authors and other relevant information. Seminar leaders should arrange to meet with me and discuss their presentations before the seminar –- ideally by the week before class. Part of this work will be to select a “model method,” for the class to explore. That is, the team leading the discussion should select an example of an exemplary use of the method in question for the rest of the class to read and think about. This should be posted to the course website a minimum of five days before the seminar. Each participant should lead once over the course of the semester. I will bring a schedule to the first class.

Participation

Participation is required for every class period. Participation will be graded as an assessment of student performance after each class. Dr. Sugg will assign a score to each student during each discussion period from 1 to 5 (1 is failing to participate in any way and 5 is participating in the fullest). If any students were lacking in participation roles, Dr. Sugg will talk to them to offer suggestions for improvement during the course. Discussion is a necessity for a graduate seminar, failure to participate in weekly discussions will result in a low participation grade. As the course progresses, more discussions will be online and feedback to your peers is required via online discussion forums and peer review feedback.

Final Research Project

Each student will complete a final project that will account for 30% of the final grade. Students should integrate feedback from the research design workshop. The research project must combine the use of quantitative methods to answer a question (or questions) concerning a Geography or Planning related topic of the student’s choosing. I suggest that you use this component to bring ideas from class into conversation with your own research focus. If you have an alternative suggestion, please feel free to talk to me about it! I am very flexible on this assignment provided it draws on work from the class but also takes it beyond the cases and readings that we have covered.

Each student will be required to have their topic approved by Dr. Sugg by the end of the third week of the semester. The final project will have two components: 1) a professional-style research paper (~10 pages) that includes Abstract, Introduction, Methodology, Results, Discussion, and Conclusion, and 2) An oral presentation of the research which will occur during the allotted final exam period. In addition, your grade will also include your evaluation of other students. This research paper will demonstrate the knowledge and effective application of quantitative research methods to a research topic selected by the student.
